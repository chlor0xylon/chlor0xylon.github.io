This research blog stood at the forefront of research in computer vision interpretability, among other related topics. I can't remember exactly how I stumbled upon it back then, and since I wasn't introduced to machine learning or even differentiable calculus at that time, I clearly remember the technicality of it all leaving me intrigued yet mostly confused at the sight of a few articles. ## Feature visualization

One of Distill's foundational articles is called _Feature Visualization_ [@olah2017featureVisualization]. It describes how neural networks designed for computer vision tasks can be probed to reverse-engineer the learned patterns cristallized into their weights after the training phase[^note-older-feature-viz].

[^note-older-feature-viz]: Even in 2017, visualizing features by creating images to maximize activations wasn't exactly new. The numerous papers cited in Distill's _Feature Visualization_ is evidence of those previous attempts, some as early as 2009, even before [AlexNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlexNet)'s breakthrough.

{/* The work carried out by Distill's authors has greatly expanded the use cases in computer vision interpretability that was pioneered along gradual developments in convolutional neural networks to explore ever more specialized feature visualization such as curve detectors [@cammarata2020curveDetectors] or spacial frequency edge detectors [@schubert2021highLowFrequencyDetectors] and using different parametrizations [@mordvintsev2018differentiableParameterizations]. */}

:::note
At the time, the Transformers architecture [@vaswani2017attention] and the [attention](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_(machine_learning)>) mechanism had just barely been conceptualized, and state-of-the-art vision models still used convolutional backbones, stemming from learnable, signal processing-inspired, convolutional filters that underpins modern computer vision. Leveraging Transformers for vision tasks wouldn't be formally introduced for another two years, until 2019 [@dosovitskiy2021vit].
:::

## Reverse-engineering visual patterns

To _visualize_ features in this context means to synthesize images by whichever means possible, comprising patterns that maximize some given activations or outputs of a neural network, in order to gain insights about what the model perceives and how it ``interprets'' the inputs. In other words it's about finding _prototypical_ images that make particular neuron groups fire in a certain way[^note-on-fire-modes].

[^note-on-fire-modes]: Said chosen groups can mean a specific neuron, or a collection of designated neurons. Distill's article _The Building Blocks of Interpretability_ [@olah2018buildingBlocks] showcases some neuron grouping choices for activation maximization.

But one of the simplest ideas to compute _prototype images_ is to treat the model as a (differentiable) black box processing its input space, and the exposed outputs. For mid-2010's-era image classification models such as Microsoft's ResNet [@he2016deepResidualLearning], the model outputs are the logits. For modern state-of-the-art ViT models, that means either patch tokens, the CLS token, register tokens [@darcet2024visionTransformersNeedRegisters], or a mix of those.

To put it plainly, let $f$ be a provided, trained, frozen neural network accepting images in (a subset of) $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^{C \times W \times H}$ in input, that returns output vectors $\mathcal{Y} \ni \mathbf{y} = f(\mathbf{x})$. To find a prototype $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ in input space for some objective function $s: \mathcal{Y} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ means to solve

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}} s(f(\mathbf{x}))
$$

which translates into ``finding an input that represents its class or target latent representation perfectly according to measure $s$''.

For example, $s$ can denote:

- the logit at index $k$: $s(\mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{y} \cdot \mathbf{e}_k$ in a classification model
- the cosine of the angle between the output CLS token and a target vector $\mathbf{y}_0$ whose semantic meaning we're interested in: $s(\mathbf{y}) = \frac{\mathbf{y} \cdot \mathbf{y}_0}{\|\mathbf{y}\|\|\mathbf{y}_0\|}$, in a general feature extractor vision model
- the activation of a specific internal neuron

In this setup, the optimized variable is the input to the frozen network, a.k.a. the data, as opposed to the training phase where the data was fixed, and drawn from a dataset, and the network's weights were the optimized variables. The point is then to use optimization techniques to synthesize input images that maximally activate specific neurons or layers.

For any neural network of more than, say, at least a few layers and a handful of input variables, such a mathematical problem is not remotely tractable due to the deep and non-linear nature of neural networks. Thus, only approximated solutions are in order. To compute one, we can resort to iterative gradient ascent, backpropagating up the network to the inputs, leveraging the the network's mapping function $f$ being differentiable. One single step of the gradient ascent process goes like this:

$$
\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)} \gets \mathbf{x}^{(t)} + \alpha \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} (s \circ f)(\mathbf{x}^{(t)})
$$

## The trials and tribulations of computing prototypes

### The problem with raw gradients

Naively deriving a solution to the above mathematical problem using the above update rule implicitely assumes that the model's gradients driving the walk in its input space are sufficiently well-behaved to stabilize around images intelligible by humans. However, neural networks usually fall short of supporting that hypothesis.

Conventional discriminative vision-based neural networks are _not_ designed to derive images back from the latent space or preserve an invertible encoding of the original inputs[^note-highly-specific-reversible-nn].

[^note-similarity-transformers-hash]: The crucial difference is that Transformers seek to preserve and reorganize information so that it is stable to local noise in the continuous variables, whereas cryptographic hashes irreversibly destroy evidence of similarity and reshuffle discrete inputs.

Rather, they are optimized to aggregate visual cues and compress information from the inputs into progressively more abstract latent representations of densely packed arrays of numbers that are supposed to be more or less invariant to semantic-preserving changes in the input space. These latents are useful for downstream prediction tasks. Forefront convolutional kernels in CNNs and Transformers patch embedding kernels usually look like Gabor filters or random noise patches [@alexnet2012; @dosovitskiy2021vit].

To me, legacy convolutional models aggregation strategy resembles an elaborate interferometry system detecting edges and alignments of visual patterns [@zeiler2014visualizingCNNs], whereas Transformer-based models' aggregation strategy of gradually refining the residual stream latents is more reminiscent of internal state buffers in cryptographic hash algorithms such as AES[^note-similarity-transformers-hash].

[^note-highly-specific-reversible-nn]: Except highly-specific architectures designed from the ground-up to also be able to possibly process the data in reverse, like Variational Auto-Encoders.

Anyway, as a matter of fact, vision models tend to backpropagate very noisy gradients in the input space, making them almost useless for reconstructing natural images. The presence of high-frequency noise in individual filters is not a proof that vision models invariably favor noisy images (because even high frequency patterns can arguably cancel out into lower frequency results) but at least it strongly hints at it. Since CNN and ViT alike seem to rely mostly on locally jittered patterns to capture the semantics of images, nothing really prevents activation maximization from falling into similarly jittered local optima, completely overriding the prototype buffer with unnaturally high frequency noise. In the field of computer vision, these images are referred to as _adversarial images_.

This poses a serious problem to gradient ascent approaches, because iteratively updating the pixels of a randomly initialized source image leads to high-frequency visual gibberish that completely ignores the reality of images taken from real-world physical scenes a.k.a. _natural images_.

## Recent advancements in computer vision feature visualization

But doing a quick literature review reveals not much has been done ever since ViTs have taken over convolutional models for vision tasks, whether supervized or unsupervized. I haven't been able to find any groundbreaking new advancements regarding computing prototypes of vision models. Most new results seem to havehappened in the late 2010's and consist in a mix of better neural networks or better regularization or improved prior modelling for the prototypes.
