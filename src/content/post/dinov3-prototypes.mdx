A few years ago, as I was wandering the internet during my bachelor years, I once came across the famous machine learning blog [Distill.pub](https://distill.pub). This research blog stood at the forefront of research in computer vision interpretability, among other related topics.  I can't remember exactly how I stumbled upon it back then, and since I wasn't introduced to machine learning or even differentiable calculus at that time, I clearly remember the technicality of it all leaving me intrigued yet mostly confused at the sight of a few articles. And more recently, I discovered it again by accident when doing a bit of literature review, so I decided to skim through it anew and catch up with the research with the benefit of hindsight, having more time and background knowledge to delve properly into the contents.


## Feature visualization
One of Distill's foundational articles is called [Feature Visualization](https://distill.pub/2017/feature-visualization/), which describes how neural networks designed for computer vision tasks can be probed to reverse-engineer the learned patterns cristallized into their weights after the training phase[^1].

[^1]: Even in 2017, visualizing features by creating images to maximize activations wasn't exactly new. The numerous papers cited in Distill's Feature Visualization is evidence of those previous attempts, some as early as 2009, even before [AlexNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlexNet)'s breakthrough.

At the time, the [Transformers architecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) and the [attention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_(machine_learning)) mechanism had just barely been conceptualized, and state-of-the-art vision models still used convolutional backbones, stemming from learnable, signal processing-inspired, convolutional filters that underpins modern computer vision. [Leveraging Transformers for vision tasks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929) wouldn't be formally introduced for another two years, until 2019.

## Reverse-engineering visual patterns

For the uninitiated, to *visualize* features, in this context, means to create images by whatever means possible, comprising patterns that maximize some given activations or outputs of a neural network, in order to gain insights about what the model perceives and how it ``interprets'' the inputs. In other words it's about finding *prototypical* images that fire particular neurons in a certain way[^2].  

[^2]: Chosen ways might target a specific neuron, or a collection of neurons. Distill's paper [The Building Blocks of Interpretability](https://distill.pub/2018/building-blocks/) showcases some neuron grouping choices for activation maximization.

But one of the simplest ideas to compute *prototype images* is to treat the model as a (differentiable) black box processing its input space, and  the exposed outputs. For mid-2010s-era image classification models such as Microsoft's [ResNet](https://arxiv.org/abs/1512.03385), that means the logits. For modern state-of-the-art ViT models, that means either image tokens, the CLS token,  [register tokens](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.16588), or a mix of those.

To put it plainly, if $f$ denotes a neural network accepting images in $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^{C \times W \times H}$ in input, and yields some output vector $\mathcal{Y} \ni \mathbf{y} = f(\mathbf{x})$, finding a prototype $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ for some objective function $s: \mathcal{Y} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$  such that

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}} s(f(\mathbf{x}))
$$

For example, $s$ can denote extracting a particular logit in a classification model, or the cosine of the angle between an objective vector whose conveyed semantic meaning we're interested in and the output CLS token, in a general vision feature extractor model such as one of Meta's DINO models family.
