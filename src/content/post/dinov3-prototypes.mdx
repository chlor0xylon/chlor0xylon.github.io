A few years ago, as I was wandering the internet during my bachelor years, I came across the well-known machine learning blog [distill.pub](https://distill.pub). This research blog stood at the forefront of research in computer vision interpretability, among other related topics. To some extent, it still does. I can't remember exactly how I stumbled upon it back then, but recently I recently discovered it again and it left me in awe, so I decided to skim through it anew and catch up with the research with the benefit of hindsight, having more time and knowledge to delve properly into the content.

One of Distill's foundational articles is called [Feature Visualization](https://distill.pub/2017/feature-visualization/), which describes how neural networks designed for computer vision tasks can be probed to reverse-engineer the learnt patterns cristallized into the network's weights after the training phase. At the time, the [Transformers architecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) and the [attention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_(machine_learning)) mechanism had just barely been conceptualized, and state-of-the-art vision models still used convolutional backbones, stemming from . [Leveraging Transformers for vision tasks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929) wouldn't be formally introduced for another two years.
