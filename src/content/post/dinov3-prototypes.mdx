This research blog stood at the forefront of research in computer vision interpretability, among other related topics.  I can't remember exactly how I stumbled upon it back then, and since I wasn't introduced to machine learning or even differentiable calculus at that time, I clearly remember the technicality of it all leaving me intrigued yet mostly confused at the sight of a few articles. ## Feature visualization
One of Distill's foundational articles is called [Feature Visualization](https://distill.pub/2017/feature-visualization/), which describes how neural networks designed for computer vision tasks can be probed to reverse-engineer the learned patterns cristallized into their weights after the training phase[^1].

[^1]: Even in 2017, visualizing features by creating images to maximize activations wasn't exactly new. The numerous papers cited in Distill's Feature Visualization is evidence of those previous attempts, some as early as 2009, even before [AlexNet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlexNet)'s breakthrough.

At the time, the [Transformers architecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) and the [attention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_(machine_learning)) mechanism had just barely been conceptualized, and state-of-the-art vision models still used convolutional backbones, stemming from learnable, signal processing-inspired, convolutional filters that underpins modern computer vision. [Leveraging Transformers for vision tasks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929) wouldn't be formally introduced for another two years, until 2019.

## Reverse-engineering visual patterns

For the uninitiated, to *visualize* features, in this context, means to create images by whatever means possible, comprising patterns that maximize some given activations or outputs of a neural network, in order to gain insights about what the model perceives and how it ``interprets'' the inputs. In other words it's about finding *prototypical* images that fire particular neurons in a certain way[^2].  

[^2]: Chosen ways might target a specific neuron, or a collection of neurons. Distill's paper [The Building Blocks of Interpretability](https://distill.pub/2018/building-blocks/) showcases some neuron grouping choices for activation maximization.

But one of the simplest ideas to compute *prototype images* is to treat the model as a (differentiable) black box processing its input space, and  the exposed outputs. For mid-2010s-era image classification models such as Microsoft's [ResNet](https://arxiv.org/abs/1512.03385), that means the logits. For modern state-of-the-art ViT models, that means either patch tokens, the CLS token,  [register tokens](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.16588), or a mix of those.

To put it plainly, let $f$ be a provided, trained, frozen neural network accepting images in (a subset of) $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^{C \times W \times H}$ in input, that returns output vectors $\mathcal{Y} \ni \mathbf{y} = f(\mathbf{x})$. To find a prototype $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ in input space for some objective function $s: \mathcal{Y} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ means to solve

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}} s(f(\mathbf{x}))
$$

which translates into ``finding an input that represents its class or target latent representation perfectly''.

For example, $s$ can denote:
- in a classification model, extracting logit of index $k$: $s(\mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{y} \cdot \mathbf{e}_k$
- in a general feature extractor vision model (such as one of Meta's DINO models family, more on that later), the cosine of the angle between the output CLS token and a target vector $\mathbf{y}_0$ whose semantic meaning we're interested in: $s(\mathbf{y}) = \frac{\mathbf{y} \cdot \mathbf{y}_0}{\|\mathbf{y}\|\|\mathbf{y}_0\|}$

In this setup, the optimized variable is the input to the frozen network, a.k.a. the data, as opposed to the training phase where the data was fixed, and drawn from a dataset, and the network's weights were the optimized variables. 


For any neural network of more than, say, at least a few layers and a handful of input variables, such a mathematical problem is not remotely tractable due to the deep and non-linear nature of neural networks. Thus, only approximated solutions are in order. To compute one, we can resort to iterative gradient ascent back propagating the gradients through the network into the inputs, leveraging the the network's mapping function $f$ being differentiable. Only then can one hope to succeed.


## Trials and tribulations of computing prototypes

The practical problem is that naively deriving a solution to the above mathematical problem (even an approached one) implicitely assumes that the driving model is sufficiently well-behaved to yield an input intelligible by humans. However, neural networks usually fall short of supporting that hypothesis.

Most neural networks were *never designed to work backward* from their latent space.[^3] Instead, they were trained to gradually build up the latent representations of input data by aggregating patterns and compressing information into densely packed arays of numbers in output.

[^3]: Except highly-specific architectures designed from the ground-up to also be able to process the data in reverse, like Variational Auto-Encoders.

As a consequence, they tend to have very noisy gradients. Even modern, SOTA vision models trained to generalize well on billions of images end up with very noisy gradients.
